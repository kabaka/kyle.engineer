Entity Categorization and Nomenclature Protocol (ENCaP)

Abstract

This document defines the Entity Categorization and Nomenclature Protocol (ENCaP), a categorization method for describing all entities in the universe as one of three classes: sandwiches, salads, or soups. The protocol provides a clear and consistent taxonomy to facilitate better understanding and communication about the nature of entities.¶

2. Introduction

The Entity Categorization and Nomenclature Protocol (ENCaP) provides a simple and consistent method for categorizing all entities in the universe into three classes: sandwiches, salads, and soups. In this document, the term 'entity' refers to any object or concept that exists in the universe and can be classified using the proposed system. By using food analogies, the ENCaP aims to facilitate better communication and understanding about the nature of these entities, as well as to provide a clear and consistent taxonomy for classification.¶

The purpose of the ENCaP is not to redefine food categories, but rather to establish a universal classification system that can be applied to any entity, whether tangible or intangible. By drawing on familiar food concepts, the ENCaP offers an approachable and intuitive method for understanding and communicating about the diverse nature of entities in the universe.¶

3. Definitions

This document uses the following definitions:¶

Sandwich
A sandwich is an entity that fully or partially surrounds, envelops, or otherwise contains one or more other entities, which can be classified as sandwiches, salads, or soups. There are two types of sandwiches: closed sandwiches and open-face sandwiches. Closed sandwiches completely or partially envelop their contents, while open-face sandwiches have contents that are not fully enveloped. Both closed and open-face sandwiches can contain sandwiches, salads, or soups as part of their composition.¶
Salad
A salad is a mixture of entities, which can be classified as solids, sandwiches, soups, or other salads, with or without a specific structure.¶
Soup
A soup is a non-solid entity, typically liquid, that does not contain solids. It is predominantly defined by its liquid nature.¶

4. Classification Rules

Entities MUST be classified into the categories of sandwiches, salads, or soups based on the following rules:¶

1. If an entity is non-solid, it SHALL be classified as a soup.¶

2. If an entity is a solid that fully or partially envelops, surrounds, or otherwise contains one or more other solids, soups, or salads, it SHALL be classified as a sandwich. Sandwiches can be of two types: those that completely enclose their contents, and open-face sandwiches, which have their contents exposed.¶

3. If an entity is a mixture of solids with optional soup, it SHALL be classified as a salad. A salad may be comprised of sandwiches, soups, or a combination thereof.¶

4. If an entity does not meet any of the above criteria, it SHALL be classified as an open-face sandwich. Open-face sandwiches may feature non-contained, related soups, salads, or other sandwiches as part of their composition.¶

5. Examples

Here are some examples to illustrate the classification rules:¶

Hamburger
A hamburger is a sandwich because it consists of a solid (bread) which partially envelops another solid (meat) and optional soup (sauce).¶
Caesar Salad
A Caesar salad is a salad because it is a mixture of solids (lettuce, croutons, cheese) with optional soup (dressing).¶
Galaxy
A galaxy is a soup because it is a non-solid entity comprised of various celestial bodies and matter dispersed throughout its volume.¶
Layered Rock Formation
A layered rock formation is a sandwich because it is a solid that partially envelops other solids (layers of different types of rock) with optional soup (water or other fluids within the layers).¶
Solar System
A solar system is an open-face sandwich because it is a solid (central star) that features non-contained, related soups (gas clouds) and salads (planets, asteroids, and other celestial bodies) as part of its composition.¶

6. Classification Procedure

This section outlines the step-by-step procedure for classifying entities, including both physical and non-physical entities, using the Entity Categorization and Nomenclature Protocol (ENCaP) classification system. The procedure is designed to help ensure consistent and accurate classification of entities as sandwiches, salads, or soups.¶

1. Determine the primary characteristics of the entity. If the entity is primarily non-solid or conceptually fluid, classify it as a soup and proceed no further.¶

2. If the entity is primarily solid or conceptually structured, evaluate its composition. If it fully or partially envelops, surrounds, or otherwise contains one or more other entities, classify it as a sandwich and proceed no further. Consider both completely enclosed and open-face sandwiches in this step.¶

3. If the entity is a mixture of elements or concepts with optional fluid components, classify it as a salad and proceed no further. A salad may be comprised of sandwiches, soups, or a combination thereof.¶

4. If the entity does not meet the criteria for soup, sandwich, or salad, classify it as an open-face sandwich. Open-face sandwiches may feature non-contained, related soups, salads, or other sandwiches as part of their composition.¶

By following this procedure, users of the ENCaP classification system can consistently and accurately classify entities, enabling clear communication and a shared understanding of the various classifications.¶
